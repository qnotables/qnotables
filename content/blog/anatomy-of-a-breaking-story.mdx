## Zero hour

The alert hits every terminal simultaneously. An event has occurred. At this moment, no one has the full picture — not the wire services, not the agencies, not the eyewitnesses still holding their phones at arm's length. What happens in the next ten minutes shapes everything that follows.

## The first draft problem

The first version of any breaking story is almost always wrong in at least one material detail. Not because the reporter is careless — because the sources are still sorting out what happened themselves. The working rule:

> The faster a story moves, the longer you should wait before you act on it.

That is not a comfortable posture in a news environment that rewards speed. But a correction issued four hours later is worse than a report filed two hours late.

## How wire copy propagates

1. **Agency breaks the story.** One outlet files. The clock starts.
2. **Syndication begins.** Within minutes, the same paragraph is running under fifty different mastheads. This looks like corroboration. It is not.
3. **Confirmation arrives.** A second *independent* source — different reporter, different city desk, different newsgathering operation — files its own account. This is the corroboration that matters.
4. **The picture fills in.** Context, background, and dissent enter the record. The original alert is now a paragraph buried in a story three times its original length.

## What we filter for

At every stage, The Wire is trying to answer the same question: *what is confirmed, by whom, and to what standard?*

- A claim sourced to a single anonymous official is labeled as such.
- A claim corroborated by two independent outlets in good standing is treated as provisional fact.
- A claim that has been walked back, corrected, or denied is flagged — the original is not silently removed.

## The patience advantage

Readers who wait an hour before forming an opinion about a breaking story are almost always better informed than those who formed one in the first five minutes. We are not asking you to disengage. We are asking you to hold the update lightly until the picture stabilizes.

That is the discipline. It is harder than it sounds.
